Data

We obtained the data from American Community Survey (ACS), which is an ongoing survey conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau. The ACS provides communities a bird’s-eye view at how they are changing over the years. The ACS collects information, such as race, age, income, commute time to work, and other important data. It’s important to note that ACS, including the commute data below, is based on a sample and is subject to sampling variability. The ACS selects 3.5 million housing unit addresses annually, across every county in the nation. This means that there is an element of statistical uncertainty in data that our visualizations are based on. The original ACS Commuting Characteristics Data defines this degree of uncertainty by including a 90% margin of error. We are 90% confident that the point estimate plus the margin of error will contain the true value. For simplicity, we decided to not include these margins of errors and solely visualize the point estimates.

First Things First: Florida Growth

Before we divulge into commute data, we must observe the marked growth of workers in Florida in recent years. The majority of counties are experiencing a growth in the workforce, which will consequently change commuting behavior. We explicitly look at the percent of workers over the age of 16 instead of population growth because we are specifically interested in the population commuting to work in a state traditionally known as a retiree state.

How long does it take to get to work?

Note how the mean travel time to work for most Florida counties are longer than the commute for the mean travel time of the average American. See the purple line where County has the longest commute in 2013. Over time, most commutes are getting progressively longer. This could be attributed to growth in working population and increased traffic congestion.

Eco-Friendly Commuters

We binned types of commutes, such as bicycling, carpooling, and telecommuting, into a category called “eco-friendly”.

Biking Commutes of Males vs Females

We also observed the relationship between gender and cycling for every county. Traditionally cycling is perceived as “too dangerous” for women. It is interesting how the male-to-female cycling ratio for Mario County, Orange County, St. Lucie County has a higher proportion of females to males biking.
